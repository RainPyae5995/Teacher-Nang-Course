RELATIVE PRONOUNS

A relative pronoun introduces an adjectival clause in a complex sentence.

Look at the following pairs of sentences. Each pair of sentences can be joined to form a single sentence.

1.
The woman carried a baby. She was my aunt.
The woman who carried a baby was my aunt.
2.
Do you know the man? He owns this shop.
Do you know the man who / that owns this shop?
3.
The woman is my neighbour. We met her.
The woman, whom / that we met, is my neighbour.
4.
This is the book. I lent it to James.
This is the book which / that I lent to James.
5.
This is the tree. Many birds make their nets here.
This is the tree where many birds make their nests.
6.
The boy is very sad. His pet has just died.
The boy, whose pet has just died, is very sad.

When the sentences are joined, the pronouns are left out. They are replaced by who, that, which, whom, where, whose.
These words are called relative pronouns.

  • Relative pronouns are words which are used as pronouns and also used to join sentences.
  • We use who and whom for people.
  • We use that for people, animals and things.
  • We use which for animals and things.
  • We use where for places.
  • We use whose to show possession.
